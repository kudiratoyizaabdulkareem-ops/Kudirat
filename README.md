# Pathways to a Low-Deforestation Future in the Democratic Republic of Congo

## Overview
This research analyzes the structural drivers of deforestation in the Democratic Republic of Congo (DRC), focusing on the interaction between agricultural expansion and demographic pressures.

Using a backcasting framework, the study moves beyond predicting future forest loss to design strategic pathways toward achieving a net-zero deforestation future by 2050.

## Research Problem
Despite its global ecological importance, the Congo Basin—particularly the DRC—faces accelerating forest loss. This study investigates:

- Why deforestation is increasing despite existing interventions (e.g., REDD+)
- How agricultural systems and population growth interact to drive forest loss
- What policy pathways are required to shift away from a business-as-usual trajectory

## Key Findings
- Agricultural expansion accounts for approximately 97% of forest loss in the DRC
- Population growth acts as a critical underlying driver, intensifying pressure on land and resources
- Current trends project continued acceleration in deforestation under a business-as-usual scenario
- Deforestation in the DRC is fundamentally linked to food security and rural livelihoods

## Methodological Approach
This study adopts a qualitative, desk-based research design combining:
- Literature review of deforestation drivers
- Spatial data analysis (Global Forest Watch, 2001–2024)
- Demographic projections (International Futures model)
- Backcasting framework to design future policy pathways

## Backcasting Framework
Rather than forecasting existing trends, this study:
1. Defines a desired 2050 future (net-zero deforestation)
2. Identifies the gap between current trajectories and that future
3. Develops strategic pathways to bridge that gap

## Strategic Pathways
### 1. Agricultural Transformation
- Shift from land expansion to intensification
- Improve land tenure security
- Strengthen rural infrastructure and value chains

### 2. Managing Demographic Pressures
- Invest in health, education, and family planning
- Promote non-farm livelihoods
- Reduce dependence on fuelwood and subsistence agriculture

## Policy Relevance
The study aligns with:
- REDD+ frameworks
- National development strategies
- Climate commitments (Paris Agreement)

It emphasizes that sustainable forest conservation must be integrated with livelihood and development policies.

## Limitations
- Reliance on secondary data
- Limited ability to capture local-level dynamics and lived experiences
- Need for future field-based validation
